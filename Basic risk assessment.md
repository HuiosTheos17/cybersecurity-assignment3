# BASIC RISK ASSESSMENT FOR A PRIVATE POLYTECHNIC


## 1.0 Introduction
Risk assessment is a systematic process used to identify valuable assets, determine threats that may affect those assets, discover vulnerabilities that may expose them to harm, evaluate the likelihood and impact of such threats, and recommend controls to reduce the risks. In modern organizations, especially educational institutions, risk assessment is very important because many activities depend on information systems, digital records, internet services, and physical infrastructure.
A private polytechnic is a suitable case study for this practical exercise because it handles a wide range of sensitive and valuable assets. These include student biodata, academic results, admission records, staff files, financial records, online registration portals, computers, local networks, and institutional documents. Since these assets support daily academic and administrative operations, any compromise can lead to disruption, financial loss, reputational damage, and possible legal consequences.
Like many small and medium-sized institutions, a private polytechnic may face threats such as hacking, phishing, malware infection, unauthorized access, theft of devices, accidental deletion of files, and damage caused by fire or power failure. These threats become more serious when the institution has vulnerabilities such as weak passwords, outdated software, lack of backups, poor access control, low security awareness, and inadequate physical protection.
This assessment focuses on identifying the major assets of a private polytechnic, examining the threats and vulnerabilities affecting them, evaluating the level of risk, and recommending practical mitigation measures. Special emphasis is placed on the results and findings of the assessment in order to show the areas of highest exposure and the controls that should be prioritized.

## 2.0 Aim
The aim of this assessment is to conduct a basic risk assessment for a private polytechnic in order to identify critical assets, determine threats and vulnerabilities, evaluate risk levels, and propose suitable mitigation strategies for protecting academic, administrative, and financial information systems.

## 3.0 Scope
This risk assessment covers the key operational and information assets of a private polytechnic. The scope includes:
Student personal and academic records 
Examination and result processing systems 
Financial records and payment data 
Staff records and administrative files 
Admission and registration systems 
School portal, databases, and network infrastructure 
Office and laboratory computers 
Physical documents and archives 
Institutional reputation and service continuity 
The assessment is limited to a basic qualitative risk assessment. It does not involve advanced technical penetration testing, forensic investigation, or full compliance auditing. Instead, it applies simple risk assessment principles to identify likely risks and control measures.

## 4.0 Objectives
The objectives of this risk assessment are to:
Identify the critical assets of a private polytechnic. 
Recognize the potential threats that may affect these assets. 
Assess the vulnerabilities that may allow threats to succeed. 
Evaluate the risk level of each identified risk based on likelihood and impact. 
Recommend mitigation strategies to reduce or manage the risks. 
Highlight the major findings and most critical areas requiring urgent attention. 

## 5.0 Methodology
The methodology used for this assessment follows a basic risk assessment approach consistent with standard guidance on risk management and cybersecurity.
### 5.1 Asset Identification
Critical assets of the polytechnic were first identified. These are the resources, systems, and information that are important to the institution’s operation and must be protected.
### 5.2 Threat Identification
Possible threats to the identified assets were then considered. These include cyber threats, human threats, operational failures, and physical hazards.
### 5.3 Vulnerability Assessment
The weaknesses that may expose the assets to threats were examined. Examples include poor password practices, obsolete software, weak access control, and inadequate backup systems.
### 5.4 Risk Evaluation
Each risk was evaluated qualitatively using two main factors:
Likelihood: the chance that the threat may occur 
Impact: the level of damage that may result if it occurs 
Risk levels were then classified as:
High Risk – very likely and/or severe effect on operations 
Medium Risk – moderate likelihood and moderate impact 
Low Risk – less likely and limited impact 
### 5.5 Mitigation Recommendation
Appropriate control measures were suggested to reduce the likelihood or impact of each risk. These controls include technical, administrative, and physical safeguards.

## 7.0 Risk Analysis and Findings
The risk assessment produced several important findings regarding the security and operational exposure of the private polytechnic.
### 7.1 Major Findings
The assessment shows that the private polytechnic has a number of high-risk assets, especially those connected to academic records, student data, result management, financial information, and the school portal. These areas are highly sensitive because they are essential to the institution’s daily operation and public credibility.
The most critical finding is that student records and examination results are among the most exposed assets. If these records are altered, deleted, leaked, or accessed by unauthorized persons, the consequences can be severe. Academic integrity may be questioned, students may lose trust in the institution, and the school’s reputation may suffer serious damage.
Another major finding is that the school portal and database systems represent a high-risk area. Since these systems support admissions, registration, fee payment, and result access, they are attractive targets for hackers and scammers. Where software updates are not regularly applied, the likelihood of compromise increases significantly.
The assessment also reveals that financial records carry a high level of risk because any weakness in access control or transaction monitoring can lead to fraud, unauthorized changes, or financial loss. The effect of such incidents is not only monetary; it may also affect accountability and confidence in the institution’s management.
A further finding is the weakness caused by poor backup arrangements. If there is no reliable backup system, the polytechnic may lose vital student, academic, and financial data through malware, accidental deletion, hardware failure, or power-related damage. This means that even a small technical incident can grow into a major operational crisis.
The assessment also found that human factors are a significant source of risk. Weak passwords, poor cyber awareness, sharing of login details, and careless handling of information increase exposure to phishing, unauthorized access, and internal misuse. This shows that risk in the institution is not only technical but also administrative and behavioral.
In addition, physical risks remain relevant. Theft of computers, fire outbreaks, flooding, and unstable power supply can interrupt operations and destroy physical or digital records. Institutions that focus only on cyber threats while ignoring physical protection may still suffer major losses.
### 7.2 Result Summary
From the assessment, the highest-risk areas are:
Student biodata and academic records 
Examination results and transcript processing 
Financial records and payment systems 
School portal, server, and database 
Power-dependent ICT infrastructure 
Institutional reputation and continuity of service 
The overall result indicates that the private polytechnic faces a moderate to high level of operational and information security risk, with several assets falling into the high-risk category.
### 7.3 Most Serious Risks Identified
The most serious risks identified are:
Unauthorized access to student and academic data 
Manipulation or loss of examination results 
Fraud or breach involving financial records 
Portal compromise through hacking or malware 
Data loss due to poor backup and unstable power supply 
Reputational damage following a breach or service failure 
### 7.4 Interpretation of Findings
These findings suggest that the private polytechnic should not treat information security as an ICT issue alone. It should be approached as an institutional management issue involving policy, people, technology, and physical infrastructure. The results clearly show that the institution’s greatest risks are concentrated around confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information.
Confidentiality risk occurs where unauthorized persons may access student, staff, or financial data. 
Integrity risk occurs where results, transcripts, or financial records may be altered improperly. 
Availability risk occurs where systems may become inaccessible due to malware, power failure, or lack of continuity planning. 
The findings therefore confirm that risk reduction should focus first on protecting sensitive records, strengthening system access, maintaining reliable backups, and improving user awareness.

## 8.0 Conclusion
This assessment has shown that a private polytechnic is exposed to a range of cyber, operational, and physical risks because it depends heavily on information assets and ICT systems for its academic and administrative activities. The identification of critical assets such as student records, examination results, financial data, staff records, and online systems reveals that the institution operates in an environment where security failures can have serious effects.
The results of the assessment indicate that the most significant risks are associated with unauthorized access, data manipulation, financial fraud, malware infection, data loss, and service disruption. These risks are made worse by vulnerabilities such as weak passwords, outdated software, inadequate access control, insufficient backup systems, low staff awareness, and unstable power supply.
Overall, the findings show that the private polytechnic has several high-risk exposure points that require prompt attention. Protecting the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of institutional data is essential not only for smooth operation but also for trust, academic credibility, and long-term sustainability.

## 9.0 Recommendations
Based on the findings of this assessment, the following recommendations are made:
The institution should enforce strong password policies for staff and system users. 
Multi-factor authentication should be introduced for important systems such as the school portal, email, and finance accounts. 
All computers, servers, and portal applications should be kept up to date through regular patching and software updates. 
Sensitive data such as student records, result files, and financial documents should be protected through role-based access control and, where possible, encryption. 
A regular backup system should be implemented, with copies kept offline or in secure cloud storage and tested for restoration. 
Staff and relevant student users should receive cybersecurity awareness training on phishing, password safety, scams, and safe data handling. 
The institution should maintain audit logs and monitoring mechanisms for critical systems, especially result processing and finance platforms. 
Shared accounts should be discouraged; each user should have a unique login identity. 
Physical security should be improved through locked offices, restricted server rooms, CCTV, and fire-resistant storage for critical records. 
Reliable power support such as UPS, inverter systems, or standby power should be provided for key ICT infrastructure. 
The polytechnic should develop a business continuity plan and incident response plan to guide recovery after cyber or physical incidents. 
Management should treat information security as a strategic issue and provide ongoing support, funding, and supervision for risk reduction activities.
